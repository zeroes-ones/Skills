### 1.2 Canvas Completion Order

Fill in the canvas in this specific sequence — it forces logic before creativity:

```
1. Customer Segments    — Who are you creating value for? (START HERE)
2. Value Propositions   — What problem do you solve for them?
3. Channels             — How do you reach them?
4. Customer Relationships — How do you get, keep, grow them?
5. Revenue Streams      — How do they pay you? (What are they really paying for?)
6. Key Resources        — What assets do you need to deliver?
7. Key Activities       — What must you do exceptionally well?
8. Key Partnerships     — Who does the rest?
9. Cost Structure       — What does it cost to run this?
```

### 1.3 Canvas Stress Tests

**Test 1: The Missing Block**
Remove one block entirely. Can the business survive? If yes, that block isn't critical —
reconsider the design.

**Test 2: The Competitor Overlay**
Draw a competitor's canvas over yours. Where are you actually different?
If your value proposition is the same, you're competing on price.

**Test 3: Narrative Coherence**
Can you tell a clear story connecting all blocks?
"These [customers] with [problem] get [value] through [channels]. We sustain [relationships] and earn [revenue] by owning [resources] and doing [activities], supported by [partners]. It costs [costs]."

**Test 4: Margin Check**
Revenue per customer − cost to serve per customer = contribution margin
If margin is negative at scale, the canvas is broken.

### 2.3 Fit Assessment

**Problem-solution fit check:**
- Each pain has at least one pain reliever
- Each gain has at least one gain creator
- Pain relievers and gain creators are RELEVANT (not just present)

**Red flags:**
- Pain relievers that address non-existent or trivial pains → no one cares
- Gain creators for gains customers don't value → wasted effort
- Too many features, none addressing critical pains → unfocused

## 3. Unit Economics

### 3.1 Core Metrics

```
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Total Sales & Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired

LTV (Customer Lifetime Value) = ARPU × Gross Margin % × Avg Customer Lifetime (months)
                               ─────────────────────────────────────────────
                                         Monthly Churn Rate

LTV:CAC Ratio = LTV / CAC
  → Target: >3:1 for SaaS (exceptional: >5:1)
  → <1:1 = you're losing money on every customer

CAC Payback Period = CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin %)
  → Target: <12 months for SMB, <18 months for enterprise
  → >24 months = capital-intensive, risky

ARPU (Average Revenue Per User) = Total Revenue / Total Users
ARPA (Average Revenue Per Account) = Total Revenue / Total Accounts

Churn Rate = Lost Customers in Period / Total Customers at Period Start
  → Monthly churn target: <2% for SMB, <1% for mid-market, <0.5% for enterprise
```

### 3.2 LTV Calculation Example

```python
# SaaS company metrics
monthly_revenue = 1_000_000
total_customers = 500
monthly_churn_rate = 0.02  # 2%
gross_margin = 0.80        # 80%

arpu = monthly_revenue / total_customers  # $2,000
avg_lifetime_months = 1 / monthly_churn_rate  # 50 months
ltv = arpu * gross_margin * avg_lifetime_months  # $80,000

cac = 5_000  # per customer
ltv_cac_ratio = ltv / cac  # 16:1 — excellent (but possibly underinvesting)
```

### 3.3 Contribution Margin

```
Unit Revenue
− Variable Costs (COGS: hosting, payment processing, support per customer)
− Variable S&M (commissions, ad costs directly attributable)
─────────────────────────────────────────
= Contribution Margin per Unit

Contribution Margin % = Contribution Margin / Unit Revenue
  → Target: >70% for SaaS, >50% for marketplace, >30% for e-commerce
```

## 4. Go-to-Market Strategy

### 4.1 GTM Model Selection

```
Decision tree:

Does the product sell itself once tried?
├── YES → Product-Led Growth (PLG)
│   Key: free tier/trial → activation → conversion → expansion
│   Metrics: activation rate, PQL-to-customer, NDR (Net Dollar Retention)
│
└── NO → Does the customer need education/convincing?
    ├── YES, and ACV < $10K → Sales-Led (Inside Sales)
    │   Key: SDR → AE → close; volume matters
    │   Metrics: pipeline generated, win rate, sales cycle, ramp time
    │
    ├── YES, and ACV $10K–$100K → Sales-Led (Field Sales)
    │   Key: relationship-driven, multi-stakeholder, long cycle
    │   Metrics: pipeline coverage (3× quota), deal size, enterprise win rate
    │
    └── YES, and ACV > $100K → Enterprise Sales
        Key: C-level relationships, custom demos/POCs, procurement
        Metrics: TAM per vertical, logo count, ACV, time-to-close

Community-Led: best for developer tools, open source, creator platforms
Partner/Channel-Led: best when you need local presence or complementary products
```

### 4.2 PLG Funnel Metrics

```
Visitors → Signups → Activated → PQL → Customer → Expanded
   │          │          │        │       │          │
   ▼          ▼          ▼        ▼       ▼          ▼
  Traffic  Conv%      Activation Product-  Win     NDR (>120%
  volume              Rate (>30%)Qualified  Rate    = best in
                               Leads       (>20%)  class)
```

**PQL (Product-Qualified Lead) definition:**
User has reached a usage threshold that historically predicts conversion:
- Reached paywall/limit: hit free tier limit
- Aha moment: completed key action that predicts retention
- Usage depth: e.g., "created 5+ projects with 2+ team members"

### 4.3 Hybrid GTM — Most Companies End Up Here

```
PLG motion (bottom-up):
  Individual users → Team adoption → Department buy-in → Enterprise expansion

Sales motion (top-down):
  C-level relationship → Department pilot → Team deployment → Individual usage

The magic: PLG generates qualified pipeline for sales; sales closes and expands PLG accounts.
```

## 5. Market Sizing

### 5.1 TAM, SAM, SOM

```
TAM (Total Addressable Market)
  └── All potential customers for your product category globally
      Example: "Global CRM market = $88B" (top-down, from analyst reports)

SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
  └── The portion of TAM you can reach with your business model & geography
      Example: "CRM for US mid-market tech companies = $12B"

SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
  └── What you can realistically capture in 3–5 years
      Example: "Year 5 target = $300M (2.5% of SAM)"
```

### 5.2 Calculation Methods

**Top-down (analyst lens):**
```
Start with industry report figure → narrow by geography, segment, use case
Pros: fast, sounds credible
Cons: often wrong, hides assumptions
```

**Bottom-up (builder lens):**
```
Count potential customers → multiply by potential revenue per customer
Example: 50,000 target companies × 200 employees × $15/user/month × 12 months
       = $1.8B SAM
Pros: grounded, defensible, reveals assumptions
Cons: time-consuming
```

**Value-based (customer lens):**
```
What's the problem worth to the customer?
Example: Current solution costs $500K/year in labor + $200K in errors.
        Our solution eliminates 80% → saves $560K.
        Customer willing to pay 1/3 of savings → $187K/customer/year.
        × 5,000 target customers = $935M SAM
Pros: value-aligned, easy for investors
Cons: willingness-to-pay uncertainty
```

### 5.3 Sizing Reality Check

**Sanity test:** Can you name 10 customers who would pay your target price?
If you can't name 10, your market might not exist yet.

**Bottom-up vs top-down gap:**
If bottom-up estimate is 10% of top-down, your top-down assumptions are too generous.
If bottom-up is 50%+ of top-down, your model might be reasonable.

**Investor red flags:**
- TAM > $100B for a pre-seed company (you don't understand focus)
- No bottom-up calculation (you haven't thought deeply)
- Market growing "because it's a $X trillion industry" (meaningless)
